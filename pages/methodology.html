Methodology

How this was built.

Source

The AI Incident Database

Our data comes from the AI Incident Database (AIID), maintained by the Responsible AI Collaborative. AIID catalogues real-world failures and harms caused by deployed AI systems — from autonomous-vehicle collisions to biased hiring algorithms to disinformation campaigns. We work from the full export, including incidents.csv, reports.csv, and the CSET v1 classification annotations.

Questions

What we set out to ask

Three questions shaped the visualizations:

  • How has the rate of reported AI incidents changed over time, and what does the recent acceleration tell us?
  • Which organizations recur most often in the incident record, and in which sectors?
  • Who bears the cost when AI systems fail, and what categories of harm dominate?
Categories

How incidents are classified

The AI Incident Database export does not include a single harm-category column. We assign each incident one primary label from seven harm-oriented categories, stored in data/incident-categories.json:

  • Discrimination & fairness — bias, disparate impact, protected characteristics
  • Privacy & surveillance — biometrics, facial recognition, privacy violations
  • Misinformation & media — deepfakes, disinformation, synthetic deception
  • Safety & autonomous systems — physical harm, AVs, robotics, clinical safety failures
  • Economic & social systems — employment, welfare, lending, criminal-justice scoring
  • Harmful content & platforms — moderation failures, hate speech, toxic amplification
  • Malicious & intentional misuse — deliberate weaponization or scam-oriented deployment

Classification runs offline via scripts/classify-incidents.py. CSET v1 annotations are applied first (~200 incidents) using a decision tree on rights violations, tangible harm, sector, AI task, and annotator notes. Remaining incidents are labeled with --openai (recommended; requires OPENAI_API_KEY) or a rubric-aligned text fallback. The dashboard only reads the committed JSON file — no API calls in the browser.

Approach

From archive to lens

We treat each incident as a single record and aggregate along three axes — time, organization, and harm category. The dashboard is built on a shared data loader that parses the CSV once and exposes a filter API so all three visualizations stay in sync as the user narrows the view.

Visualizations are rendered with D3.js. The site itself is plain HTML/CSS/JavaScript — no framework, no build step.

Sustainability

Why this matters for sustainable AI

Sustainable AI is often framed in terms of compute and carbon. We argue for a wider definition: a system is sustainable only if the institutions deploying it can learn from its failures. The incident record is the feedback signal that closes that loop — what gets catalogued, gets addressed.

Making that record visible and queryable is, in our view, a piece of the long-term infrastructure responsible AI development depends on.
